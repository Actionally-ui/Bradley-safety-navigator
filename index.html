Watch for early signs

  • Mumbling or slurred speech.
  • Removing footrests early.
  • Repetitive roster questions.

Do this

  • Have the vehicle and equipment ready before he moves.
  • Distract and redirect early.
  • Keep language short and concrete.

Live Decision

Use brief language. Keep moving forward.

Say this

“Bradley, we’ve got to head off now. Your appointment’s at [time]. Let’s get going.”
“The car’s here to take us. We’ll be quick and then we can [preferred activity].”

Do this

  • Avoid explaining rules, systems, or insurance.
  • Do not argue or justify.
  • Redirect to time, destination, or preferred activity.

Pathway A

Skills deficit / duty of care.

Say this

“Bradley, your legs are too shaky today. It isn’t safe to transfer right now.”

Do this

Pause the outing and move to an in-home or lower-demand option if he agrees.

Record this

Shift notes: “Step 7 – Health/Safety Pause”.

Then

Move to recovery and dignity. Do not debrief the incident.

Result

Not a restrictive practice.

Pathway B

Behaviour / restrictive practice.

Say this

State the risk briefly and do not argue further.

Do this

Prevent exit to maintain safety if he refuses to wait and attempts to proceed anyway.

Report this

Incident report: “Unauthorised Restrictive Practice”.

Then

Move to recovery and dignity when safe.

Result

Environmental restraint. Reportable incident.

Short phrases for real-time use.

Escalation / refusal

“Bradley, we’ve got to head off now. Your appointment’s at [time]. Let’s get going.”

Vehicle transition

“The courtesy bus is here to take us to the cafe. Let’s head out.”

Mobility check

“Let’s just test the legs on the machine first.”

Distraction

“Did you see that boat on the news?”

Recovery

Preserve dignity after the event.

  • Do not debrief the incident with Bradley.
  • Move straight to a preferred, low-demand activity.
  • Act as if everything is normal.
Bradley values being the boss of his own life. Respect his identity, even in recovery.

More Info

Optional extra detail only.

About Bradley
Bradley values dignity, respect, humour, directness, and being treated like an equal. Waiting, overload, and loss of control can increase frustration.
Why behaviour may escalate
Triggers can include the walking belt, delays, fatigue, unfamiliar staff, and challenges to his beliefs in the moment.
Proactive strategies
Keep a zero-wait approach, use visual cues over argument, and use humour, banter, and relational scaffolding.
Skill building
Use in-the-moment experiential feedback rather than debate. Reframe transport respectfully and reduce waiting wherever possible.
