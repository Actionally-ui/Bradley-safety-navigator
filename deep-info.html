Understanding Bradley

Deep background and communication approach

Who is Bradley?

Bradley is a 66-year-old man who values dignity, respect, and being the “boss” of his own life. He takes pride in his history as a business owner, carpenter, surfer, and abalone diver. He has impressive upper-body strength and is a talented storyteller who loves having a laugh with people who treat him as an equal.

  • Loves nature, especially Currumbin Valley.
  • Enjoys visiting cafes and engaging in banter with familiar staff.
  • Responds well to humour, warmth, and being treated as an equal.
  • Values honesty and direct communication.
  • Dislikes waiting, too much noise, or too many instructions.

How His Brain Injury Affects Him

Bradley has a brain injury that affects his memory and how he processes information. Understanding these impacts is crucial for staff interactions.

  • Body vs. Brain: His brain tells him he can walk or transfer easily (“I can do it”), but his legs are not steady enough. This creates genuine confusion, not stubbornness.
  • Memory Loss: He might forget that he needs help with balance, or that a plan such as a taxi was discussed earlier.
  • Confabulation: He may fill memory gaps with other memories he believes to be true.
  • Overload: Too much noise, too many instructions, or too much waiting can overwhelm him and trigger escalation.
Key insight: Bradley is not being difficult. His brain is genuinely telling him something different from reality. Arguing or using logic will not change his perception in the moment.

Why Behaviour May Escalate

Bradley’s escalation stems from grief, loss of identity, and lack of insight into his physical deficits.

  • Grief and Loss: He has lost his pre-injury identity as an independent business owner and physical labourer. This creates frustration.
  • Lack of Insight: He genuinely cannot perceive his physical deficits in the moment. He believes he is still capable.
  • Loss of Control: The walking belt feels like “someone grabbing the steering wheel while I’m driving.”
  • Fatigue and Sundowning: Afternoon fatigue leads to increased confusion and verbal aggression.
  • Unfamiliar Staff: He may mask vulnerability with new people by acting overly independent, which can increase impulsive behaviour.
Critical trigger: Challenging his beliefs or using logic and evidence when he is dysregulated will escalate him further.

Communication Approach

Bradley responds best to a communication style that respects his identity and reduces cognitive load.

  • Treat him like an equal: Do not boss him around.
  • Use humour and banter: He engages well with people who joke with him and treat him with warmth.
  • Be direct but kind: Tell him straight, but with respect.
  • Validate emotion without correcting confabulation: If he believes something false, do not argue. Redirect instead.
  • Use neutral phrasing: “Courtesy bus” or “courtesy transport” may feel less confronting.
  • Avoid verbal logic when dysregulated: Keep scripts short and forward-moving.

Why Scripts Are Brief

The scripts in the Safety Navigator are intentionally short. This is because Bradley cannot process long explanations when dysregulated.

  • Arguing or over-explaining increases escalation.
  • Bradley cannot process verbal logic when his brain is in crisis mode.
  • Short, forward-moving prompts redirect attention and reduce cognitive load.
  • Visual cues are often more effective than words.
  • Staff need to stay calm and brief to model regulation.
Remember: The goal is not to convince Bradley. The goal is to keep him safe and move forward.

Proactive Strategies

These strategies prevent escalation before it starts.

  • Zero Wait Rule: Have the vehicle ready, doors open, equipment positioned, and shoes or wallet ready before inviting Bradley to move.
  • Visual Cues Over Verbal Argument: If Bradley insists he is steady but is not, do not argue. Bring the Sara Stedy and let the equipment speak for itself.
  • Experiential Feedback: Let him feel balance limits safely, then name what happened.
  • Respectful Reframing: Use language that supports dignity rather than disability-coded language.
  • Waiting Tolerance Support: Use distraction such as a preferred video if waiting cannot be avoided.

Bradley’s Escalation Cycle

Understanding the stages helps staff recognise where Bradley is and respond appropriately.

1. Baseline
Calm, storytelling, joking, engaging with staff as an equal. Staff response: engage in stories, validate his role, keep mood light.
2. Early Warning
Mumbling, removing footrests, repetitive questions about roster or missing staff. Staff response: distract, redirect, change subject, check physical needs.
3. Escalation
Swearing, accusing staff, refusing the walking belt, asserting control. Staff response: use direct scripts, reduce verbal clutter, give space, stop arguing logic.
4. Crisis
Attempting to stand without support, pushing wheelchair near traffic, high-volume shouting, incoherent outbursts. Staff response: stop the transfer, focus on safety, use Step 7.
5. Recovery
Fatigue, falling asleep, apology, memory loss regarding the incident. Staff response: do not debrief, move to preferred activity, act as if everything is normal.

Duty of Care vs. Restrictive Practice

This distinction is central to the Safety Navigator.

  • Duty of Care (Pathway A): Stopping a transfer because Bradley fails screening due to physical inability and agrees to wait or change the plan.
  • Restrictive Practice (Pathway B): Stopping a transfer because Bradley refuses safety instructions and staff must prevent exit to maintain safety.
The key difference: If Bradley agrees to the pause, it is duty of care. If he refuses and staff must physically prevent him from leaving, it is a restrictive practice.

Reporting Requirements

Accurate reporting protects Bradley and supports compliance.

  • Pathway A: Record in shift notes as “Step 7 – Health/Safety Pause”.
  • Pathway B: Report as an incident using “Unauthorised Restrictive Practice”.
Remember: Accurate reporting is accountability and protection.

Recovery & Dignity

After an incident, staff response shapes Bradley’s recovery and future trust.

  • Do NOT debrief: Avoid discussing the incident with Bradley.
  • Move immediately to a preferred activity: Go straight to coffee, TV, or another low-demand activity.
  • Act as if everything is normal: Behave naturally to preserve dignity.
  • Respect his identity: Recovery is an opportunity to reinforce that respect.

Key Takeaways

  • Bradley’s behaviour is driven by grief, loss of identity, and neurological changes.
  • He cannot process verbal logic when dysregulated. Keep scripts short and forward-moving.
  • Proactive strategies such as zero wait, visual cues, and experiential feedback reduce escalation.
  • The Step 7 decision tool helps staff distinguish between duty of care and restrictive practice.
  • Recovery is about preserving dignity and moving forward, not blame.
  • Treat Bradley like an equal. Use humour, banter, and respect.
