Live Decision

Use brief language. Keep moving forward. Avoid debates and explanations.

Agitation / escalation

“Bradley, we’ve got to head off now. Your appointment’s at [time]. Let’s get going.”
“The car’s here to take us. We’ll be quick and then we can [preferred activity].”
“Let’s head out now, we’re running on time.”

Staff reminders

  • Avoid explaining rules, systems, or insurance.
  • Do not argue or justify.
  • Keep language short and concrete.
  • Redirect to time, destination, or preferred activity.
  • If helpful, frame transport as courtesy transport or a courtesy bus.

Safety Pause

Manual transfers only. Stop, focus on safety, then choose the pathway.

Step 7

  1. Stop the transfer.
  2. Focus on safety.
  3. Use the decision tool below.
Pathway A: Skills deficit
  • Physical issue such as fatigue, balance, or confusion.
  • Bradley agrees to wait or change plan.
  • Outcome: duty of care.
Tap to follow the Pathway A sequence
Pathway B: Behaviour
  • Refusal, aggression, or unsafe actions.
  • Bradley attempts to proceed anyway.
  • Outcome: restrictive practice and reportable incident.
Tap to follow the Pathway B sequence

Important distinction

  • Pathway A = person agrees to wait.
  • Pathway B = person refuses and movement is restricted.

Pathway A sequence

Use this when the issue is physical instability, fatigue, confusion, or poor balance.

Trigger

Bradley fails the OT screening due to incapacity such as fatigue, disorientation, or poor balance.

Interaction

“Bradley, your legs are too shaky today. It isn’t safe to transfer right now.”

Consent

Bradley agrees to wait, postpone, or do an indoor activity.

Staff action

Implement an in-home activity. Door is not blocked. He chooses to stay.

Result

Not a restrictive practice. Negotiated duty-of-care decision based on a skills deficit.

Then do this

  • Move to the reporting section and record the correct wording.
  • Then move to recovery and dignity.

Pathway B sequence

Use this when Bradley refuses to wait and attempts to proceed anyway.

Trigger

Bradley fails the screening due to behaviours of concern such as aggression, impulsivity, or refusal to follow safety instructions despite capacity.

Interaction

Staff explain the risk, but Bradley escalates, yells, or attempts to force his way past staff to the vehicle.

Consent

Bradley refuses to wait and attempts to leave anyway.

Staff action

Staff prevent exit to maintain safety, for example blocking the door, locking the gate, or refusing to unlock the car.

Result

Environmental restraint. This is a reportable incident and an unauthorised restrictive practice.

Then do this

  • Move to the reporting section and use the restrictive practice wording.
  • Then move to recovery and dignity.

Duty of care vs restrictive practice

  • Duty of care: physical inability, person agrees, plan adjusts.
  • Restrictive practice: behaviour refusal, person does not agree, movement is blocked or restricted.

Recovery & dignity

Preserve respect after the event.

  • Do not debrief the incident with Bradley.
  • Move straight to a preferred, low-demand activity.
  • Act as if everything is normal to preserve dignity.
Core principle: Bradley values being the boss of his own life. Respect his identity, even in recovery.

Deeper plan: About Bradley

More in-depth context from the comprehensive plan.

Identity and strengths

  • Values dignity, respect, and being the boss of his own life.
  • Takes pride in his history as a business owner, chippy, surfer, and abalone diver.
  • Strong upper body, talented storyteller, enjoys banter and nature.

Support needs

  • Brain injury affects memory and information processing.
  • May believe he can walk or transfer safely when his legs are not steady.
  • Too much noise, instruction, or waiting can overwhelm him.

What works

  • Treat him like an equal.
  • Use humour.
  • Be direct but kind.
  • Have everything ready before mentioning the outing.

Deeper plan: Why this happens

Functional analysis summary for staff who need more context.

Predisposing factors

  • Grief and loss between pre-injury identity and post-injury reality.
  • Need for control and autonomy.
  • Lack of insight into physical deficits in the moment.
  • May confuse new memories with older memories and believe they are true.

Triggers

  • The walking belt, which he calls the strap.
  • Waiting and delays.
  • Cognitive fatigue, especially later in the day.
  • Unfamiliar staff.

What keeps it going

  • Inconsistent feedback after unsafe transfers.
  • Staff preventing falls can reduce his awareness of instability.
  • Blaming staff keeps frustration directed at people rather than the situation.

Protective factors

  • Relational connection, warmth, humour.
  • Trust in family members more than staff for some transport decisions.
  • Storytelling about past competence can regulate mood.

Deeper plan: Proactive strategies

These are the preventative strategies under the comprehensive plan.

1. Framing

  • He rejects personal authority but responds better to service-based framing.
  • Courtesy transport wording may help for WAV use.

2. Zero wait rule

  • Have the vehicle arrived and open before he moves.
  • Have equipment positioned before he stands.
  • Have shoes and wallet ready before mentioning the outing.

3. Visual cues over argument

  • Do not argue when he insists he is steady.
  • Use equipment to show instability safely.
  • Keep language short and concrete.

4. Relational scaffolding

  • Use humour and banter.
  • Do not correct confabulation in the moment.
  • Validate the feeling and redirect to a preferred topic.

Deeper plan: Skill building

These can sit underneath the script section as optional extra detail.

Real-time safety insight

Use experiential feedback rather than argument. Let him feel the balance limit safely, then name the wobble.

Vehicle transfer tolerance

Reframe transport as service or courtesy. Reduce disability-coded language and use visual anchors where appropriate.

Waiting tolerance

Set up fully before inviting movement. Where waiting is unavoidable for WAV, use video distraction.

Deeper plan: Escalation cycle

Expanded stage-by-stage reference from the comprehensive plan.

1. Baseline / calm

  • Storytelling, joking, banter, tabletop games, nature or yachting content.
  • Values being treated as an equal and making choices.

2. Early warning signs

  • Mumbling, slurred speech, unease at shift start.
  • Removing footrests, repetitive questioning, fixation on staff or roster.

3. Escalation

  • Swearing, accusing staff, refusing belt or vehicle, asserting boss role.
  • Staff response: reduce verbal clutter, do not argue, redirect forward.

4. Crisis / peak

  • Impulsive standing, pushing wheelchair near hazards, incoherent shouting, threats.
  • Staff response: immediate safety and Step 7 decision.

5. Recovery

  • Fatigue, confusion, apology, withdrawal, or no recollection.
  • Do not debrief. Return to a low-demand preferred activity.
