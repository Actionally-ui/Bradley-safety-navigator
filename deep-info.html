Understanding Bradley

Deep background and communication approach

Who is Bradley?

Bradley is a 66-year-old man who values dignity, respect, and being the "boss" of his own life. He takes pride in his history as a business owner, carpenter, surfer, and abalone diver. He has impressive upper-body strength and is a talented storyteller who loves having a laugh with people who treat him as an equal.

  • Loves nature, especially Currumbin Valley
  • Enjoys visiting cafes and engaging in banter with familiar staff
  • Responds well to humour, warmth, and being treated as an equal
  • Values honesty and direct communication
  • Dislikes waiting, too much noise, or too many instructions

How His Brain Injury Affects Him

Bradley has a brain injury that affects his memory and how he processes information. Understanding these impacts is crucial for staff interactions.

  • Body vs. Brain: His brain tells him he can walk or transfer easily ("I can do it"), but his legs aren't steady enough. This creates genuine confusion, not stubbornness.
  • Memory Loss: He might forget that he needs help with balance, or that a plan (like a taxi) was discussed earlier.
  • Confabulation: He may fill memory gaps with other memories he believes to be true (e.g., believing he paid a cleaner $340).
  • Overload: Too much noise, too many instructions, or too much waiting can overwhelm him and trigger escalation.
Key insight: Bradley is not being difficult. His brain is genuinely telling him something different from reality. Arguing or using logic will not change his perception in the moment.

Why Behaviour May Escalate

Bradley's escalation stems from grief, loss of identity, and lack of insight into his physical deficits.

  • Grief and Loss: He has lost his pre-injury identity as an independent business owner and physical labourer. This creates deep frustration.
  • Lack of Insight: He genuinely cannot perceive his physical deficits in the moment. He believes he is still capable.
  • Loss of Control: The walking belt feels like "someone grabbing the steering wheel while I'm driving." It triggers a sense of powerlessness.
  • Fatigue and Sundowning: Afternoon fatigue leads to increased confusion and verbal aggression.
  • Unfamiliar Staff: He masks vulnerability with new people by acting overly independent, leading to impulsive behaviour.
Critical trigger: Challenging his beliefs or using logic/evidence when he is dysregulated will escalate him further.

Communication Approach

Bradley responds best to a specific communication style that respects his identity and reduces cognitive load.

  • Treat him like an equal: Don't "boss him around." Use the "Employer" stance—frame rules as business protocols, not personal demands.
  • Use humour and banter: He engages well with people who joke with him and treat him with warmth.
  • Be direct but kind: Tell him straight, but with respect. Avoid long explanations or justifications.
  • Validate emotion without correcting confabulation: If he believes something false, don't argue. Instead, redirect or distract.
  • Use neutral phrasing: Say "courtesy bus" instead of "disability van." Say "courtesy transport" instead of "maxi-taxi."
  • Avoid verbal logic when dysregulated: He cannot process complex explanations when upset. Keep scripts short and forward-moving.

Why Scripts Are Brief

The scripts in the Safety Navigator are intentionally short. This is not because staff lack information—it's because Bradley cannot process long explanations when dysregulated.

  • Arguing or over-explaining increases escalation
  • Bradley cannot process verbal logic when his brain is in crisis mode
  • Short, forward-moving prompts redirect his attention and reduce cognitive load
  • Visual cues (showing the Sara Stedy, opening the car door) are more effective than words
  • Staff need to stay calm and brief to model regulation
Remember: The goal is not to convince Bradley. The goal is to keep him safe and move forward.

Proactive Strategies

These strategies prevent escalation before it starts. They should be implemented before any behaviour occurs.

  • Zero Wait Rule: Have the vehicle ready, doors open, equipment positioned, and shoes/wallet ready before inviting Bradley to move. Waiting creates a vacuum for impulsivity.
  • Visual Cues Over Verbal Argument: If Bradley insists he is steady but is not, don't argue. Bring the Sara Stedy and let the equipment speak for itself.
  • Experiential Feedback: Instead of telling him "You are wobbling," let him feel his balance limits safely on the Sara Stedy, then say "See that wobble? That's why we need the support today."
  • Respectful Reframing: Stop using words like "Disability Van" or "Maxi-Taxi." Use "courtesy bus" to support his dignity.
  • Waiting Tolerance Support: If Bradley must wait for a WAV, hand him a phone playing a video (e.g., boat racing) so his attention is on the screen, not the wait.

Bradley's Escalation Cycle

Understanding the stages helps staff recognize where Bradley is and respond appropriately.

1. Baseline
Calm, storytelling, joking, engaging with staff as an equal. Staff response: Engage in stories, validate his role as "the boss," keep mood light.
2. Early Warning
Mumbling, removing footrests, repetitive questions about roster or missing staff. Staff response: Distract and redirect, change subject, check physical needs.
3. Escalation
Swearing, accusing staff of laziness, refusing the walking belt, asserting control. Staff response: Use direct scripts, reduce verbal clutter, give space, stop arguing logic.
4. Crisis
Attempting to stand without support, pushing wheelchair near traffic, high-volume shouting, incoherent outbursts. Staff response: Stop the transfer, focus on safety, use Step 7 decision tool.
5. Recovery
Fatigue, falling asleep, apology, memory loss regarding the incident. Staff response: Do not debrief, move to preferred activity, act as if everything is normal.

Duty of Care vs. Restrictive Practice

This distinction is central to the Safety Navigator. Understanding it protects Bradley and guides staff decisions.

  • Duty of Care (Pathway A): Stopping a transfer because Bradley fails the OT screening due to physical inability (fatigue, poor balance, disorientation). Bradley agrees to wait or change the plan. This is NOT a restrictive practice—it's a negotiated safety decision based on skills deficit.
  • Restrictive Practice (Pathway B): Stopping a transfer because Bradley refuses to follow safety instructions and attempts to proceed anyway. Staff prevent his exit (blocking door, locking gate) to maintain safety. This IS a restrictive practice and must be reported as an incident.
The key difference: Does Bradley agree to the pause? If yes, it's duty of care. If he refuses and staff must physically prevent him from leaving, it's a restrictive practice.

Reporting Requirements

Accurate reporting protects Bradley and ensures compliance with NDIS requirements.

  • Pathway A (Duty of Care): Record in shift notes as "Step 7 – Health/Safety Pause." No incident report required.
  • Pathway B (Restrictive Practice): Report as a reportable incident with wording "Unauthorised Restrictive Practice." This must be lodged with the NDIS Commission.
Remember: Accurate reporting is not punishment—it's accountability and protection for Bradley.

Recovery & Dignity

After an incident, the way staff respond shapes Bradley's recovery and future trust.

  • Do NOT debrief: Avoid discussing the incident with Bradley. He may have no recollection, and talking about it can re-traumatise him.
  • Move immediately to a preferred activity: Go straight to coffee, TV, or another low-demand activity he enjoys.
  • Act as if everything is normal: Behave naturally to preserve his dignity. Do not treat him differently or apologise repeatedly.
  • Respect his identity: Bradley values being the "boss" of his own life. Recovery is an opportunity to reinforce that respect.

Key Takeaways

  • Bradley's behaviour is driven by grief, loss of identity, and neurological changes—not stubbornness or malice.
  • He cannot process verbal logic when dysregulated. Keep scripts short and forward-moving.
  • Proactive strategies (zero wait, visual cues, experiential feedback) prevent escalation before it starts.
  • The Step 7 decision tool helps staff distinguish between duty of care and restrictive practice.
  • Accurate reporting protects Bradley and ensures compliance.
  • Recovery is about preserving dignity and moving forward, not debriefing or blame.
  • Treat Bradley like an equal. Use humour, banter, and respect. He responds to warmth and direct communication.
